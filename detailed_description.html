  • GERD Health & Fitness

    Aims

    The overall aim of our project is to create a well-rounded mobile application that will be of assistance towards sufferers of gastroesophageal reflux disease. Meanwhile, smaller goals consist of Informing the app’s users about helpful information on GERD, helping them to establish exactly what types of foods/drinks they personally can and cannot consume, and to allow our users to observe and monitor their nutritional intake.

    When a person is first diagnosed with gastroesophageal reflux disease, they will be given some information and general advice from their doctor, although it is often found to require a lot of further research later on, particularly towards exactly what foods are and are not acidic, but also other helpful tips such as standing whilst eating, drinking a glass of water before each meal, and including plain rice cakes into your diet (which will soak up excess acid in the stomach). For this reason, an important part of a well-rounded mobile application to assist sufferers is an educational tab.
    The educational tab will include many informative features, alongside a search bar, where you can input your favorite foods, and it will return information on both whether you can eat those foods, and what their nutritional value is. Informing users on helpful information is a back-bone goal towards our overall goal and a sure way to aid users in their recovery and avoidance of further symptoms/damage to their bodies.
    The second most important thing for helping a person recovering from/living with GERD is for the person to be able to accurately determine what foods they can and cannot eat. Now, controversially to the educational tab, everyone is different, as are the number and severity of symptoms, and exactly what foods/drinks they can and cannot consume. Because of this, tracking symptoms will play an important role in their recovery, and therefore the diet and symptoms tracker was a crucial part of our mobile application.
    The diet and symptoms tracker will do exactly this; helping the user to establish exactly what types of foods/drinks they personally can and cannot consume. This will be done using a diet diary, which will act as a record of what the person has consumed throughout their day/week, a symptoms tracker (which will act as a recording of the persons symptoms), and pop-up advice generated from the programmed analysis of the persons diet and symptoms. With thanks to the users’ data inputs and the programming then being able to take that data and analyze it, the application will then be able to generate personalized feedback based on these results. This feedback will be able to tell the user things such as “Your symptoms have been higher than average, here are the foods we suspect of causing these issues: _____”
    Now, with such an ambitious application, creating it all within a shortened time from can certainly have its limits. Therefore, our team has had to approach this with the consideration of importance over what parts of the application are more important than others and should therefore take precedence. Whilst the three major sub-topics themselves are all very important parts of the overall goals for our project, there are some particulars that can be cut down where required.

    Plans and Progress

    Our team has a common interest and motivation in our desire to help people in need. Because of this, the opportunity to create an app that could potentially do exactly this was too good to pass up. A member of the team, Jordan Glen, is personally affected by GERD, and has undergone quite a tough time dealing with it, including almost daily pains, regurgitation, nausea, and many of the other symptoms of GERD, as well as having to change his diet and liquid consumption completely, and even undergo investigative surgery in order to finally be diagnosed after having suffered with it for seven years before then, untreated.

    GERD is a condition that has a wide array of symptoms, and yet (albeit sometimes requiring the assistance of medication or further surgery) changes in food and liquid consumption can have a drastically positive affect on the reduction of these symptoms, and the ability to allow the body to heal before further damage is done. If the person fails to do this they can end up with progressively worsening symptoms and the growth of ulcers on their stomach (Danisa M. Clarrett, MD 2018).

    In addition to this, it can affect the bodyweight of a person suffering from GERD, and as such, many sufferers will go on to set fitness goals, that without very specific diet advise, unfortunately will be extremely difficult, sometimes impossible to achieve. The internet and world of phone applications in the present day is vast, however, to the best of our research, there are no applications nor websites that combine all of the Diet, Symptom and Fitness tracking features in a Mobile App

    The GERD Health & Fitness app will deliver a one-stop solution for its users to learn general information about GERD, regularly track their Diet and Symptoms, create a personalised Nutritional Planner, and provide a Fitness Progress Tracker.

    Knowledge is key, particularly when it comes to medical issues, which is why we have decided that it is essential for our first tab to be a more informative one, bearing searchable information on what is, GERD, what symptoms to look out for, and more importantly, offering numerous pieces of advice to the user that can help reduce damage to their body and alleviate symptoms.

    For added convenience, this tab will also include the option to search for numerous types of foods, which will return both nutritional information regarding this food, as well as whether it is a known trigger for GERD symptoms, and how acidic that item is on a scale of one to five.

    The diet diary would be a diary that can be filled in once a day, or to the users liking, that gathers data from the user, used to store and assess the information. The data gathered will be the foods and liquids consumed that day. An important part to this diary would also be the optional ability to add a diagnosis date. This date will be saved, and later considered when processing the information.

    The symptoms tracker would operate similarly to the diet diary in the way of also gathering and storing data obtained from the user, however, it would be more appropriately displayed in a checklist form for the purposes of gathering symptoms, meaning that data will then have consistent data feedback in the back end of the program, without having multiple variations of the same symptoms written, nor symptoms that have nothing to do with GERD. This will be essential to refer back to for the advice section of this process. Perhaps a symptom strength chart would be necessary as well. It may also be necessary to gather this information daily or weekly. The time frames being mentioned will help when planning when to send notifications to the user.

    The next function to be written (in terms of programming functions) will be the pop-up advice section. This will take the data gathered from both the diet diary and the symptoms tracker and assess dietary information over the last two weeks (the period necessary for all the diet related symptoms to show (Colleen M. Story 2017)). If the person has had numerous symptoms within that time period, it will collect the data taken from the diet diary and move it to a separate function that will add the GERD provocative foods consumed to a “possible” list. If foods within this list continue to match up regularly with symptoms, then it will move them from the ‘possible’ list and into the ‘likely’ list and be highlighted to the user.

    **It is helpful to note that the pop-up advice, in terms of its programming function, will also both receive and send data regarding the third tab; GERD-Based Nutritional Planner. **

    The pop-up advice will appear on two occasions; when the user has been having issues with either symptoms, not meeting their nutritional requirements (this can be handled gently by subtly suggesting certain foods for the user to include in their diet and displaying the benefits of those foods) or having difficulties reaching their set out goals. The second occasion will be when the user is new to the app and having a look around. This will be a good opportunity to provide the user with as much useful information as possible.

    If GERD provocative foods are eaten a number of times with no symptoms shown afterwards, then those specific foods will be moved to the ‘okay_to_eat’ list. The more foods that are gathered in this list, the more accurate the information can be towards discovering which foods to place in the ‘possible’ or ‘likely’ lists. For this reason, the ‘okay_to_eat’ list will need priority over the ‘possible’ and ‘likely’ lists.

    The third tab, being the ‘GERD Based Nutritional Planner’, is a section designed to assist the user in the monitoring of the nutritional side of their diet, along with the utility of optional assistance should they wish to embark upon a fitness/health journey as well (with thanks to the previous reference made regarding GERD also affecting a person's body weight).

    The nutritional planner will be generated as a few different sections within the tab, consisting of a mix of user input and tables with automated calculations to display. These sections will include meal nutrition calculators, a daily totals calculator, and the utility section which tracks the users progress on their personal fitness journey. To do so, upon initial use a pop-up will be displayed, collecting important data such as the persons age, gender, weight, height, fitness goals, previous history of fitness goals and how they went, and their exercise type/amount.

      Testing

      The success or failure of a mobile application can often come down to how robust the application is during its initial launch cycle. Applications that are technically sound and are well maintained with timely updates are likely to remain above their competitors on both Google Play and Apple’s App Store. Likewise, applications that perform poorly, are prone to crashes or other technical glitches, or fail to innovate through updates are more likely to be suppressed in search results (BitBar, 2022). Further, a poorly designed application can lead to the short-term failure of an application, and potentially long-term reputational damage to a developer. Thankfully, the thorough testing of an application prior to launch can help to mitigate many of the common issues that plague new application releases. The following section will outline the steps our team will be taking during the testing phase of development in the hopes of ensuring our launch is as smooth as possible.

      There are generally considered to be two broad disciplines or streams of software testing. The first is ‘automated’ testing and consists of testing carried out by software tools. Automated testing is considered to be more accurate and reliable compared with ‘manual’ testing due to its high repeatability. Further, it is often significantly faster and more efficient than other forms of testing, especially in instances that require large-scale load testing, the testing of repetitive tasks, and use-cases that require the simulation of hundreds or thousands of simultaneous users. However, despite boasting speed and accuracy benefits, automated testing lacks the ability to conduct more subjective aspects of an application such as user-experience and design aesthetics (Apica, 2017).

      With this in mind, the testing process for our application will be conducted in two stages. The first stage will be conducted during the course of the development of the application and will be largely automated using ‘Appium’. Appium is described as an “open-source test automation framework for use with native, hybrid and mobile web apps” (Appium, 2022). Appium has been chosen as our automated testing solution as it is capable of testing applications written in Python for both iOS and Android platforms. This stage of testing will commence as early as possible during the development phase as it will help detect flaws and glitches in our software before they compound and become increasingly difficult to fix.

      The second stage of testing will become increasingly manual and will involve the use of focus groups sourced directly through GERD-related Facebook groups and other online communities. This stage will commence much closer to the initial launch of the application and will assist in ensuring that the application is user-friendly and meets the expectations of our user-base. Further, manual testing will also provide much-needed insights into potential issues surrounding unexpected software crashes, hardware combability problems, memory leaks, and security vulnerabilities (Smith, 2022).
      Alongside these two formal testing stages, the development team will regularly conduct a series of informal tests. These tests will be specifically designed to help provide an insight into how the application impacts a mobile device’s battery life, and how the software interacts with hardware sensors including in-built gyroscopes, ambient light sensors, temperature sensors, etc (Thakkar, 2020).

      FEATURE-CREEP

      One other potential risk that could negatively impact our development process is the phenomenon of ‘feature-creep’. Feature-creep or ‘scope-creep’ refers to the adding of “excessive features to a product that make it too complicated or difficult to use” (Babich 2022). In other words, feature-creep occurs when an application attempts to become a ‘jack-of-all-trades’ by poorly implementing too many features. Feature-creep is seen as one of the most common problems plaguing software development, and generally stems from “poor planning, insufficient product strategy, and misaligned priorities. Typically, requests for new features are added after the project has started, are out of scope, and the changes are not properly reviewed” (Babich 2022).

      With this in mind, our team is taking steps to ensure that feature-creep does not negatively impact our project. For example, we are directing all lines of communication through a single application – Microsoft Teams – and ensuring that all meetings follow a strict agenda system. This ensures that any major decisions regarding changes or additions to features within the application are heard by the whole team prior to implementation. Likewise, any proposed changes will be reviewed against the original scope and objectives of our application by the entire development team. This will confirm that any proposals remain within the original scope of the project while leaving room for new ideas and creativity.

      MISGUIDED DEVELOPMENT DIRECTIONS

      Issues regarding the overall direction of development often stem from a disconnect between the developer and their target audience. This leads software developers to implement unnecessary or unwanted features and can be another contributing factor to the risks surrounding ‘feature-creep’ described above.

      In order to combat the potential issue of misguided development direction, our team is dedicating a great deal of time during the initial planning stages to ensuring that the project scope remains tightly aligned with our target audience. To assist with this, we plan on regularly asking ourselves a few key questions throughout the development of the project:
      1. What is the fundamental reason for developing the application?
      2. What feature/s make our application different from competing applications?
      3. Who is the application being developed for?

      If, at any point in time during the development of the application, our answers to the above questions stray too far from the answers given at the stages of planning, we will pause development and reassess our goals against the original project scope.
      Further, once our application nears completion, we will reach out to our primary demographic and ask two more key questions:

      1. Does the application add value to the user?
      2. Is the application effective and efficient to use?

      Alongside these questions, the team will look to use focus groups and will take on feedback from end-users during beta testing, which will further ensure that the project stays closely aligned with user expectations and mitigates any potential for misguided development direction.

      INSECURE DATA STORAGE (BACKUPS & VIRUS)

      The possibility of data loss, either through hard drive failure or through malicious computer virus could have a devastating impact on our project as it could potentially wipe out weeks or months of work. Thankfully, while this risk poses a very real threat to anyone developing software, there are a number of simple steps that can be taken to drastically minimise the negative consequences of data loss.

      For example, our development team will be utilising a combination of cloud-based storage solutions and hard drives to store all working documents, code blocks, and digital designs. All code and programming-related information will be stored in a single GitHub repository, accessible to the entire team. At the same time, all individual programmers will be required to maintain two local copies of any code blocks that they are working on. Likewise, all development documents such as meeting notes, development plans, and visual design plans, will be stored both in a Dropbox account accessible to the entire team, as well as saved in two separate locations locally by the project’s Lead Developer.

      By using a combination of cloud-based storage and hard-disk drive storage solutions, our team is adequately protected from any instances of data loss by ensuring that there is no single point of failure.

      BLOWN-OUT DEVELOPMENT TIMELINE

      While our team will continuously endeavour to meet any deadlines that we set, there is always the risk of our development timeline getting blown-out due to unforeseen circumstances. Many of the likely causes for major delays in production are detailed above, however there is always the potential for unforeseen circumstances to negatively impact our project.

      While some issues may be unavoidable, we believe that the best way that we can ensure that development remains on-track as much as possible is to closely stick to the communication practices outlined in the ‘Group Processes’ section of this document. Further, it is important to note any combination of the risks outlined above may occur at the same time, creating an exponentially negative impact on the development process. With this in mind, it is essential that our development team closely adheres to the mitigation steps outlined above.

      Group processes and communications

      One of the challenges that we face is that all four members of our team are spread across Australia, meaning that face-to-face meetings would be impossible. In response to this, we looked into a number of different online platforms in the hopes of finding a single application that could handle the scheduling and recording of online meetings, facilitate a group chat for wider communications, private messaging for conversations between individual team-members, and file-sharing to allow for document collaboration. With this checklist in mind, we looked at a variety of options, including Slack, Facebook Messenger, Zoom, and Microsoft Teams. While all of these platforms offered something a bit different, only Microsoft Teams met all of our requirements.

      As a result, Microsoft Teams will be the exclusive medium for communication used by our team as we develop our project. Any group-wide communications including meeting notifications and requests for progress updates will be posted in the main Teams channel for all members to see. Team members will also use the private messaging function within Teams for any one-to-one communications. This is to ensure that all project-related communications – whether private or otherwise – remain within a single application as opposed to spreading communications across multiple platforms. Likewise, all meetings will be conducted using Microsoft Teams and will be recorded to allow team-members to review meetings if required.

      The team will endeavour to meet twice per week. Once on a Monday evening to discuss action points for the upcoming week, and once towards the end of the week to provide a progress update. All meetings will be scheduled at a minimum of 48 hours in advance of the meeting’s start-time to ensure that team members have ample time to arrange to be available. As stated above, all meetings will be recorded and can be viewed by any team member who cannot make a particular meeting.

      While we will strive to ensure regular communication at all times, there will undoubtedly be issues where people are unavailable or when they are unable to participate in group activities for a variety of reasons. With this in mind, we will always attempt to be as reasonable as possible when dealing with breaks in communication from individual members. For example, if a team member fails to turn up to a meeting without giving prior notice, they will be contacted privately with any updates from the meeting and will be asked if there is a more suitable time to meet so that – if possible – the group can accommodate to their schedule. More often than not, simply reaching out and offering this kind of assistance and support is all that is needed to resolve any communication issues. However, in serious cases where a team member is repeatedly missing meetings without notice and fails to respond to multiple attempts to contact them privately, they will be advised that they face expulsion from the group. After three more attempts to make contact over the course of two weeks, the member will be removed from the group and a replacement will be sought.
